Guangzhou Daily:The Sir Percival David Ceramics Collection – Equal to the Palace Museums’ Collections

30 June 2014 Reporter: Yuejun Jiang


“The British Museum Chinese Ceramics: Highlights of the Sir Percival David Collection” Chinese Version Front Page

Following the opening of the British Museum’s new bookshop in its central hall, the Chinese version of “The British Museum Chinese Ceramics: Highlights of the Sir Percival David Collection” has become the first ever foreign language work to be sold inside the museum. During interview Yuanyuan Tan, CEO of the publisher of this book, Art Asia, and visiting lecturer at Sotheby’s Institute explained how this book came to be published in Chinese and the value and significance of Sir Percival David’s collection.

The British Museum was established in the mid-18th century and is one of the best museums in the world in terms of the number, breadth and type of its collections as well as in terms of its influence. Other than Beijing’s Palace Museum and Taiwan’s National Palace Museum the British Museum has the most important Chinese art collection in the world with almost 30,000 items.

According to Ms. Tan the British Museum’s permanent exhibition only includes about 2,000 Chinese artefacts which are displayed in Room 33 (China, South Asia and Southeast Asia) to which was added last year the permanent borrowed exhibition in Room 91 (Sir Percival David Chinese Ceramics) which is the largest Chinese Imperial Kiln ceramics collection outside of China. The British Museum’s other Chinese artefacts are all kept in storage and are not displayed. Prior to the publication of the Chinese version of “The British Museum Chinese Ceramics: Highlights of the Sir Percival David Collection” The British Museum had never published any books on Chinese artefacts in Chinese. In 2011, having got in touch with former Curator of Chinese paintings in the Department of Oriental Antiquities at the British Museum, Dr Anne Farrer and Jan Stuart, Keeper of the Department of Asia, British Museum and under the influence of the Dr Tao Wang, then lecturer at SOAS and now Head of the Chinese Works of Art Department at Sotheby’s New York, Ms. Tan started to gather and organise information about Chinese national treasures that had made their way to overseas museums and launched Art Asia’s professional cooperation with The British Museum. “The British Museum Chinese Ceramics: Highlights of the Sir Percival David Collection” is the first result of this cooperation, and is also the first Chinese publication on this collection.

Yuan Dynasty Blue and White “David Vases”

Sir Percival David (1892-1964) has been named the greatest of Chinese art collectors. He collected almost 1,700 pieces of Chinese ceramics, the vast majority are of which are guan yao treasures from past dynasties and standard objects bearing important informative inscriptions, including the best enamel porcelain collection outside of China which is only second to the Taipei National Palace Museum’s ruyao Collection as well as Yuan Dynasty Blue and White which has been named by the ceramics world after Sir Percival David as the “David Vases”. Today America’s Metropolitan Museum’s treasure “Night-Shining White” by Han Gan of the Tang Dynasty, also comes from Sir Percival David’s collection.

According to the recollections of Sir Percival David’s second wife, Sir Percival David started to collect Chinese ceramics from the age of 22, and he learnt Chinese and studied ancient China’s literature in order to understand the inscriptions on the ceramics. In 1924 Sir Percival David went to China for the first time, and in 1925 he was invited to help design a palace art treasures exhibition inside the Forbidden City. During 1927-1928 Sir Percival David went to Beijing again just at the time when the Qing dynasty had collapsed and many palace treasures were finding their way on to the market. Rumour being that when the Express Ci Xi left the palace in 1901 she had pawned several hidden treasures to Beijing’s salt bank, interested persons started to turn their attention to these objects. Likewise through patience, clever socialising and miraculous luck, Sir Percival David eventually bought over 40 porcelain treasures which he shipped back to London in 3 separate shipments. During 1930-1931, whilst organizing and hosting all sorts of Imperial treasure art exhibitions, he also collected, through all sorts of means, large numbers of precious ceramic treasures. At the beginning of the 20th century, his close to 1,700 ceramics, which date from the 10th to the 18th centuries, became a milestone for the entire Western world’s Chinese ceramics collection, and to this day remain the largest and most complete single collection outside of the Palace Museums in Beijing and Taipei.

“The British Museum Chinese Ceramics: Highlights of the Sir Percival David Collection” highlights 50 groups of ceramics from almost 1,700 objects of this world-renowned collection.
